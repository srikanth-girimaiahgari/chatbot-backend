Live Milestones

Foundation

Multi-tenant DM routing became real

The original goal was simple: one system, many businesses, no product or FAQ mix-up between them. This is the layer that turned DigiMaya from a single bot into a service platform.
Live
20% logic Without clean tenant separation, nothing above it can be trusted. This step made DigiMaya operational at the business-account level.

What we intended

  • One backend should serve multiple businesses.
  • Each business should only use its own products and FAQs.
  • Instagram messages should route to the correct business automatically.

What went live

  • Tenant-based Instagram DM routing.
  • Tenant-specific product and FAQ replies.
  • Provider dashboard support for more than one business.

What changed from the idea

  • Instagram became the strongest first channel.
  • Website chat was deliberately deprioritized.
  • WhatsApp stayed secondary because Meta setup created friction.
Operations

Provider dashboard and Google Sheet monitoring

The plan was not only to make MAYA reply, but to make DigiMaya operable as a service. This became the internal control room.
Live
20% logic If the service provider cannot see health, activity, and setup status, the product is not manageable at scale.

What we intended

  • One place to monitor all businesses.
  • Simple health and activity visibility.
  • Google Sheets sync for lightweight reporting.

What went live

  • Admin dashboard with overview, tenants, activity, and handoffs.
  • Google Sheets export endpoint.
  • Hourly Sheets trigger for recurring updates.

What changed from the idea

  • Provider dashboard became the source of truth.
  • Google Sheets became a reporting layer, not the core system.
Client Layer

Client signup, onboarding, and branded portal

The goal was to stop doing everything manually for each business. DigiMaya needed a client-facing workspace that felt like a real SaaS product.
Live
20% logic A service becomes a product when onboarding, settings, and day-to-day visibility stop depending on the builder doing everything by hand.

What we intended

  • Clients should sign up and log in themselves.
  • Clients should add products, FAQs, hours, and profile details.
  • The portal should feel branded and product-like.

What went live

  • Client signup, login, logout, onboarding, and settings.
  • Editable catalog and FAQ management.
  • Admin-first, client-second activation flow.
  • Branded DigiMaya entry screen and lavender UI system.

What changed from the idea

  • Manual Meta setup stayed in the loop for now.
  • The portal evolved from form-heavy to brand-first entry design.
  • Client dashboard stayed business-focused, not technical.
Commerce Quality

Lead protection, product links, and onboarding friction reduction

Once the product was live, the next need was quality: better product replies, better lead handling, and less manual effort for the client.
Live
20% logic A business assistant becomes useful when it protects time, avoids dumb loops, and helps move real customer intent forward.

What we intended

  • Hot leads should be flagged.
  • Low-value assistant loops should stop.
  • Products should support links and images.
  • Clients should import products more easily.

What went live

  • Hot lead alerts after strong intent or 10+ turns.
  • Anti-loop protection for repetitive low-information chats.
  • Product URL and image URL support.
  • Bulk product import by pasted lines in the client portal.

What changed from the idea

  • Lead alerts stayed useful, but no longer define the whole sales flow.
  • The bigger goal shifted from “notify seller” to “remove seller from routine flow.”

Digital Employee Milestones

Digital Employee Step 1

Structured order intents

This is the first step where DigiMaya starts acting like a digital employee, not just a pre-sales assistant. Purchase-ready conversations are turned into structured business records.
Live
20% logic Before DigiMaya can create payment links or confirm orders, it needs a clean internal object for purchase-ready work. That object is the order intent.

What we intended

  • Move beyond loose handoffs.
  • Capture purchase-ready details in a structured way.
  • Start removing the seller from the normal lead path.

What went live

  • New order_intents SQL migration.
  • Instagram and WhatsApp purchase-intent capture into structured records.
  • Admin and client dashboard visibility for order-intent counts and lists.
  • Draft-order layer now runs on top of order intents.

What changed from the idea

  • Order intent replaced the old idea of loose lead handoff for purchase-ready conversations.
  • Seller notification is no longer the normal destination for a standard buying flow.
  • Payments are still intentionally held back until the ownership model is clear.
Digital Employee Step 2

Draft orders from purchase-ready DMs

The next logical move after order intent is to let DigiMaya prepare a real draft order. This is the first point where the system begins organizing an actual transaction object, even before payment is added.
Live
20% logic Payment should not happen before DigiMaya has a proper order object. Draft orders are the bridge between “customer wants to buy” and “customer is being charged correctly.”

What we intended

  • Turn structured order intent into a real draft order.
  • Keep seller out of the normal path unless something is unclear.
  • Prepare DigiMaya for payment links later without jumping too early.

What went live

  • New orders SQL migration for draft orders.
  • Automatic draft-order creation from structured order intents.
  • Admin and client dashboard metrics for draft orders.
  • Draft orders are enriched with matched product, currency, and amount fields when a clear product match exists.
  • The model was corrected from single-product orders toward cart-style order items.
  • Multi-item pricing now uses separate order items instead of multiplying one unit price across different products.

What changed from the idea

  • The first order model was too simple because it assumed one product per order.
  • The correct commerce shape became: one order header, many order items.
  • Payment-link generation is still held back even though payment-ready fields now exist.
Digital Employee Step 3

Payment-link automation is prepared but intentionally paused

The next layer is to turn a valid draft order into an external payment link. The code path has been explored locally, but this is the first high-risk money step, so it is being held until payment-account ownership is defined correctly for multiple clients.
Held
20% logic DigiMaya should never collect money into the wrong account. Before payment links go live, each business must have a clear payment-owner model so customer money goes to the correct merchant, not into an operational gray area.

What we intended

  • Create payment links from safe draft orders.
  • Send the link back in the DM conversation.
  • Move DigiMaya from order preparation into real checkout execution.

What is prepared so far

  • Draft orders now carry payment-ready fields like amount, currency, and payment status.
  • The next payment-link code path has already been explored locally with Razorpay.
  • The team now understands that payment must be tied to the correct business owner, not one shared account.

Why it is held

  • Multi-client payment ownership must be designed correctly first.
  • Each business should ideally own its own payment account.
  • Money-handling mistakes are a much bigger risk than messaging mistakes.
Digital Employee Step 4

Shopify foundation starts with schema, not prompts

The next expansion is Shopify, but the first serious step is not teaching MAYA new words. It is giving DigiMaya the right tenant-level store connection fields, product-to-variant mapping, cart tracking, and webhook audit structure so the backend can be tested cleanly before AI is wired into it.
In Development
20% logic Shopify checkout depends on one key truth: DigiMaya cannot add a product to a Shopify cart unless it knows the correct Shopify variant for that tenant. That makes schema design the real first step, not conversation design.

What we intended

  • Connect DigiMaya to Shopify one step at a time.
  • Keep MAYA decoupled from raw Shopify API logic.
  • Make cart and checkout work per tenant, not as one shared store shortcut.

What is built first

  • Tenant-level Shopify connection fields.
  • Product-to-Shopify variant mapping fields.
  • shopify_carts and shopify_cart_items tables.
  • shopify_webhook_events table so later HMAC-verified webhooks have a proper audit path.
  • An isolated Shopify backend service for fetch products, create cart, update cart, and get checkout URL.
  • Protected admin endpoints to test Shopify calls without involving MAYA yet.
  • A Shopify webhook verification scaffold that can reject fake events and log verified ones.

What is intentionally untouched

  • No MAYA prompt change yet.
  • No Shopify API calls wired into production flow yet.
  • No checkout execution yet.
  • No webhook closure yet.
Digital Employee Step 5

MAYA now asks for checkout in structured intent, not raw Shopify logic

This is the first shopping-behavior layer. MAYA can now signal checkout intent in structured JSON, but the backend still decides whether Shopify checkout is actually possible. That keeps the AI layer decoupled from store API details.
In Development
20% logic If Shopify API details live inside the prompt, every store change becomes a prompt problem. If MAYA only describes intent and the backend executes it, DigiMaya stays maintainable.

What we intended

  • Let MAYA behave like a shopping employee.
  • Keep MAYA decoupled from Shopify API changes.
  • Use structured intent, not hard-coded checkout instructions in natural language.

What is built so far

  • MAYA can now end with SHOPPING_INTENT_JSON when Shopify checkout is available for the tenant.
  • The backend parses that JSON and decides whether to create a Shopify checkout.
  • Shopify checkout now resolves products directly from Shopify catalog data instead of relying on local Supabase product mapping as the primary path.
  • DigiMaya still syncs the resulting Shopify cart back into local orders and order items for operations visibility.
  • The old order-intent flow stays in place as the fallback.

What is intentionally still missing

  • No production Shopify checkout has been proven yet because there is still no real store connected.
  • No customer-facing fulfillment or paid-order confirmation yet.
  • No Shopify order-completion logic off webhook topics yet.
Digital Employee Step 6

Shopify checkout is prepared first, then confirmed only by webhook

DigiMaya now treats Shopify checkout and Shopify success as two different truths. A checkout link only means the cart is ready. A verified Shopify webhook is what can finally move the local order into a real confirmed state.
In Development
20% logic Sending a checkout link is not proof of payment. If DigiMaya says “confirmed” too early, the business loses money and the customer gets the wrong promise. So webhook verification must be the only thing that closes the sale.

What we corrected

  • Linked Shopify carts now push local orders into checkout_ready.
  • Linked carts keep the order’s original session id and channel instead of drifting to request values.
  • Shopify carts now carry DigiMaya attributes like tenant id, order id, and session id.

What is built now

  • Verified Shopify webhooks can look for digimaya_order_id inside order attributes.
  • Matched orders can now move to order_created or confirmed depending on the Shopify event.
  • Local orders now store Shopify order id, Shopify order number, and confirmation time.

What still remains

  • The live end-to-end DM path still needs one full proof run against the Shopify sandbox.
  • Webhook topic handling will need more polish for abandoned or unpaid checkouts later.
  • Customer confirmation messages should only be added after the webhook-based success path is proven cleanly.
